## Maxim 1 — Chapter I: LAYING PLANS, v1
Sun Tzŭ said: The art of war is of vital importance to the State.

## Maxim 2 — Chapter I: LAYING PLANS, v2
It is a matter of life and death, a road either to safety or to ruin. Hence it is a subject of inquiry which can on no account be neglected.

## Maxim 3 — Chapter I: LAYING PLANS, v3
The art of war, then, is governed by five constant factors, to be taken into account in one’s deliberations, when seeking to determine the conditions obtaining in the field.

## Maxim 4 — Chapter I: LAYING PLANS, v4
These are: (1) The Moral Law; (2) Heaven; (3) Earth; (4) The Commander; (5) Method and discipline.

## Maxim 5 — Chapter I: LAYING PLANS, v5, 6
The Moral Law causes the people to be in complete accord with their ruler, so that they will follow him regardless of their lives, undismayed by any danger.

## Maxim 6 — Chapter I: LAYING PLANS, v7
Heaven signifies night and day, cold and heat, times and seasons.

## Maxim 7 — Chapter I: LAYING PLANS, v8
Earth comprises distances, great and small; danger and security; open ground and narrow passes; the chances of life and death.

## Maxim 8 — Chapter I: LAYING PLANS, v9
The Commander stands for the virtues of wisdom, sincerity, benevolence, courage and strictness.

## Maxim 9 — Chapter I: LAYING PLANS, v10
By Method and discipline are to be understood the marshalling of the army in its proper subdivisions, the gradations of rank among the officers, the maintenance of roads by which supplies may reach the army, and the control of military expenditure.

## Maxim 10 — Chapter I: LAYING PLANS, v11
These five heads should be familiar to every general: he who knows them will be victorious; he who knows them not will fail.

## Maxim 12 — Chapter I: LAYING PLANS, v14
By means of these seven considerations I can forecast victory or defeat.

## Maxim 13 — Chapter I: LAYING PLANS, v15
The general that hearkens to my counsel and acts upon it, will conquer:—let such a one be retained in command! The general that hearkens not to my counsel nor acts upon it, will suffer defeat:—let such a one be dismissed!

## Maxim 14 — Chapter I: LAYING PLANS, v16
While heeding the profit of my counsel, avail yourself also of any helpful circumstances over and beyond the ordinary rules.

## Maxim 15 — Chapter I: LAYING PLANS, v17
According as circumstances are favourable, one should modify one’s plans.

## Maxim 16 — Chapter I: LAYING PLANS, v18
All warfare is based on deception.

## Maxim 17 — Chapter I: LAYING PLANS, v19
Hence, when able to attack, we must seem unable; when using our forces, we must seem inactive; when we are near, we must make the enemy believe we are far away; when far away, we must make him believe we are near.

## Maxim 18 — Chapter I: LAYING PLANS, v20
Hold out baits to entice the enemy. Feign disorder, and crush him.

## Maxim 19 — Chapter I: LAYING PLANS, v21
If he is secure at all points, be prepared for him. If he is in superior strength, evade him.

## Maxim 20 — Chapter I: LAYING PLANS, v22
If your opponent is of choleric temper, seek to irritate him. Pretend to be weak, that he may grow arrogant.

## Maxim 21 — Chapter I: LAYING PLANS, v23
If he is taking his ease, give him no rest.

## Maxim 22 — Chapter I: LAYING PLANS, v24
Attack him where he is unprepared, appear where you are not expected.

## Maxim 23 — Chapter I: LAYING PLANS, v25
These military devices, leading to victory, must not be divulged beforehand.

## Maxim 24 — Chapter I: LAYING PLANS, v26
Now the general who wins a battle makes many calculations in his temple ere the battle is fought.

## Maxim 26 — Chapter II: WAGING WAR, v2
When you engage in actual fighting, if victory is long in coming, the men’s weapons will grow dull and their ardour will be damped. If you lay siege to a town, you will exhaust your strength.

## Maxim 27 — Chapter II: WAGING WAR, v3
Again, if the campaign is protracted, the resources of the State will not be equal to the strain.

## Maxim 28 — Chapter II: WAGING WAR, v4
Now, when your weapons are dulled, your ardour damped, your strength exhausted and your treasure spent, other chieftains will spring up to take advantage of your extremity. Then no man, however wise, will be able to avert the consequences that must ensue.

## Maxim 29 — Chapter II: WAGING WAR, v5
Thus, though we have heard of stupid haste in war, cleverness has never been seen associated with long delays.

## Maxim 30 — Chapter II: WAGING WAR, v6
There is no instance of a country having benefited from prolonged warfare.

## Maxim 31 — Chapter II: WAGING WAR, v7
It is only one who is thoroughly acquainted with the evils of war that can thoroughly understand the profitable way of carrying it on.

## Maxim 32 — Chapter II: WAGING WAR, v8
The skilful soldier does not raise a second levy, neither are his supply-waggons loaded more than twice.

## Maxim 33 — Chapter II: WAGING WAR, v9
Bring war material with you from home, but forage on the enemy. Thus the army will have food enough for its needs.

## Maxim 34 — Chapter II: WAGING WAR, v10
Poverty of the State exchequer causes an army to be maintained by contributions from a distance. Contributing to maintain an army at a distance causes the people to be impoverished.

## Maxim 35 — Chapter II: WAGING WAR, v11
On the other hand, the proximity of an army causes prices to go up; and high prices cause the people’s substance to be drained away.

## Maxim 36 — Chapter II: WAGING WAR, v12
When their substance is drained away, the peasantry will be afflicted by heavy exactions.

## Maxim 37 — Chapter II: WAGING WAR, v13, 14
With this loss of substance and exhaustion of strength, the homes of the people will be stripped bare, and three-tenths of their incomes will be dissipated;

## Maxim 38 — Chapter II: WAGING WAR, v15
Hence a wise general makes a point of foraging on the enemy. One cartload of the enemy’s provisions is equivalent to twenty of one’s own, and likewise a single picul of his provender is equivalent to twenty from one’s own store.

## Maxim 39 — Chapter II: WAGING WAR, v16
Now in order to kill the enemy, our men must be roused to anger; that there may be advantage from defeating the enemy, they must have their rewards.

## Maxim 40 — Chapter II: WAGING WAR, v17
Therefore in chariot fighting, when ten or more chariots have been taken, those should be rewarded who took the first. Our own flags should be substituted for those of the enemy, and the chariots mingled and used in conjunction with ours. The captured soldiers should be kindly treated and kept.

## Maxim 41 — Chapter II: WAGING WAR, v18
This is called, using the conquered foe to augment one’s own strength.

## Maxim 42 — Chapter II: WAGING WAR, v19
In war, then, let your great object be victory, not lengthy campaigns.

## Maxim 43 — Chapter II: WAGING WAR, v20
Thus it may be known that the leader of armies is the arbiter of the people’s fate, the man on whom it depends whether the nation shall be in peace or in peril.

## Maxim 44 — Chapter III: ATTACK BY STRATAGEM, v1
Sun Tzŭ said: In the practical art of war, the best thing of all is to take the enemy’s country whole and intact; to shatter and destroy it is not so good. So, too, it is better to capture an army entire than to destroy it, to capture a regiment, a detachment or a company entire than to destroy them.

## Maxim 45 — Chapter III: ATTACK BY STRATAGEM, v2
Hence to fight and conquer in all your battles is not supreme excellence; supreme excellence consists in breaking the enemy’s resistance without fighting.

## Maxim 46 — Chapter III: ATTACK BY STRATAGEM, v3
Thus the highest form of generalship is to baulk the enemy’s plans;

## Maxim 47 — Chapter III: ATTACK BY STRATAGEM, v4
The rule is, not to besiege walled cities if it can possibly be avoided.

## Maxim 49 — Chapter III: ATTACK BY STRATAGEM, v6

## Maxim 50 — Chapter III: ATTACK BY STRATAGEM, v7
With his forces intact he will dispute the mastery of the Empire, and thus, without losing a man, his triumph will be complete.

## Maxim 51 — Chapter III: ATTACK BY STRATAGEM, v8
It is the rule in war, if our forces are ten to the enemy’s one, to surround him; if five to one, to attack him;

## Maxim 52 — Chapter III: ATTACK BY STRATAGEM, v9
If equally matched, we can offer battle;

## Maxim 53 — Chapter III: ATTACK BY STRATAGEM, v10
Hence, though an obstinate fight may be made by a small force, in the end it must be captured by the larger force.

Now the general is the bulwark of the State: if the bulwark is complete at all points; the State will be strong; if the bulwark is defective, the State will be weak.

But when the army is restless and distrustful, trouble is sure to come from the other feudal princes. This is simply bringing anarchy into the army, and flinging victory away.

## Maxim 57 — Chapter III: ATTACK BY STRATAGEM, v17
Thus we may know that there are five essentials for victory: (1) He will win who knows when to fight and when not to fight.

## Maxim 58 — Chapter III: ATTACK BY STRATAGEM, v18
Hence the saying: If you know the enemy and know yourself, you need not fear the result of a hundred battles. If you know yourself but not the enemy, for every victory gained you will also suffer a defeat.

## Maxim 59 — Chapter IV: TACTICAL DISPOSITIONS, v1
Sun Tzŭ said: The good fighters of old first put themselves beyond the possibility of defeat, and then waited for an opportunity of defeating the enemy.

## Maxim 60 — Chapter IV: TACTICAL DISPOSITIONS, v2
To secure ourselves against defeat lies in our own hands, but the opportunity of defeating the enemy is provided by the enemy himself.

## Maxim 61 — Chapter IV: TACTICAL DISPOSITIONS, v3
Thus the good fighter is able to secure himself against defeat,

## Maxim 62 — Chapter IV: TACTICAL DISPOSITIONS, v4
Hence the saying: One may know how to conquer without being able to do it.

## Maxim 63 — Chapter IV: TACTICAL DISPOSITIONS, v5
Security against defeat implies defensive tactics; ability to defeat the enemy means taking the offensive.

## Maxim 64 — Chapter IV: TACTICAL DISPOSITIONS, v6
Standing on the defensive indicates insufficient strength; attacking, a superabundance of strength.

## Maxim 65 — Chapter IV: TACTICAL DISPOSITIONS, v7
The general who is skilled in defence hides in the most secret recesses of the earth;

## Maxim 66 — Chapter IV: TACTICAL DISPOSITIONS, v8
To see victory only when it is within the ken of the common herd is not the acme of excellence.

## Maxim 67 — Chapter IV: TACTICAL DISPOSITIONS, v9
Neither is it the acme of excellence if you fight and conquer and the whole Empire says, "Well done!"

## Maxim 68 — Chapter IV: TACTICAL DISPOSITIONS, v10
To lift an autumn hair is no sign of great strength;

## Maxim 69 — Chapter IV: TACTICAL DISPOSITIONS, v11
What the ancients called a clever fighter is one who not only wins, but excels in winning with ease.

## Maxim 70 — Chapter IV: TACTICAL DISPOSITIONS, v12
Hence his victories bring him neither reputation for wisdom nor credit for courage.

## Maxim 71 — Chapter IV: TACTICAL DISPOSITIONS, v13
He wins his battles by making no mistakes.

## Maxim 72 — Chapter IV: TACTICAL DISPOSITIONS, v14
Hence the skilful fighter puts himself into a position which makes defeat impossible, and does not miss the moment for defeating the enemy.

## Maxim 73 — Chapter IV: TACTICAL DISPOSITIONS, v15
Thus it is that in war the victorious strategist only seeks battle after the victory has been won, whereas he who is destined to defeat first fights and afterwards looks for victory.

## Maxim 74 — Chapter IV: TACTICAL DISPOSITIONS, v16
The consummate leader cultivates the moral law, and strictly adheres to method and discipline; thus it is in his power to control success.

## Maxim 75 — Chapter IV: TACTICAL DISPOSITIONS, v17
In respect of military method, we have, firstly, Measurement; secondly, Estimation of quantity; thirdly, Calculation; fourthly, Balancing of chances; fifthly, Victory.

## Maxim 76 — Chapter IV: TACTICAL DISPOSITIONS, v18
Measurement owes its existence to Earth; Estimation of quantity to Measurement; Calculation to Estimation of quantity; Balancing of chances to Calculation; and Victory to Balancing of chances.

## Maxim 77 — Chapter IV: TACTICAL DISPOSITIONS, v19
A victorious army opposed to a routed one, is as a pound’s weight placed in the scale against a single grain.

## Maxim 78 — Chapter IV: TACTICAL DISPOSITIONS, v20
The onrush of a conquering force is like the bursting of pent-up waters into a chasm a thousand fathoms deep. So much for tactical dispositions.

## Maxim 79 — Chapter V: ENERGY, v1
Sun Tzŭ said: The control of a large force is the same principle as the control of a few men: it is merely a question of dividing up their numbers.

## Maxim 80 — Chapter V: ENERGY, v2
Fighting with a large army under your command is nowise different from fighting with a small one: it is merely a question of instituting signs and signals.

## Maxim 81 — Chapter V: ENERGY, v3
To ensure that your whole host may withstand the brunt of the enemy’s attack and remain unshaken—this is effected by manœuvers direct and indirect.

## Maxim 82 — Chapter V: ENERGY, v4
That the impact of your army may be like a grindstone dashed against an egg—this is effected by the science of weak points and strong.

## Maxim 83 — Chapter V: ENERGY, v5
In all fighting, the direct method may be used for joining battle, but indirect methods will be needed in order to secure victory.

## Maxim 84 — Chapter V: ENERGY, v6
Indirect tactics, efficiently applied, are inexhausible as Heaven and Earth, unending as the flow of rivers and streams; like the sun and moon, they end but to begin anew; like the four seasons, they pass away but to return once more.

## Maxim 85 — Chapter V: ENERGY, v7
There are not more than five musical notes, yet the combinations of these five give rise to more melodies than can ever be heard.

## Maxim 86 — Chapter V: ENERGY, v8
There are not more than five primary colours (blue, yellow, red, white, and black), yet in combination they produce more hues than can ever be seen.

## Maxim 87 — Chapter V: ENERGY, v10
In battle, there are not more than two methods of attack—the direct and the indirect; yet these two in combination give rise to an endless series of manœuvers.

## Maxim 88 — Chapter V: ENERGY, v11
The direct and the indirect lead on to each other in turn. It is like moving in a circle—you never come to an end. Who can exhaust the possibilities of their combination?

## Maxim 89 — Chapter V: ENERGY, v12
The onset of troops is like the rush of a torrent which will even roll stones along in its course.

## Maxim 90 — Chapter V: ENERGY, v13
The quality of decision is like the well-timed swoop of a falcon which enables it to strike and destroy its victim.

## Maxim 91 — Chapter V: ENERGY, v14
Therefore the good fighter will be terrible in his onset, and prompt in his decision.

## Maxim 92 — Chapter V: ENERGY, v15
Energy may be likened to the bending of a crossbow; decision, to the releasing of the trigger.

## Maxim 93 — Chapter V: ENERGY, v16
Amid the turmoil and tumult of battle, there may be seeming disorder and yet no real disorder at all; amid confusion and chaos, your array may be without head or tail, yet it will be proof against defeat.

## Maxim 94 — Chapter V: ENERGY, v17
Simulated disorder postulates perfect discipline; simulated fear postulates courage; simulated weakness postulates strength.

## Maxim 95 — Chapter V: ENERGY, v18
Hiding order beneath the cloak of disorder is simply a question of subdivision;

## Maxim 96 — Chapter V: ENERGY, v19
Thus one who is skilful at keeping the enemy on the move maintains deceitful appearances, according to which the enemy will act.

## Maxim 97 — Chapter V: ENERGY, v20
By holding out baits, he keeps him on the march; then with a body of picked men he lies in wait for him.

## Maxim 98 — Chapter V: ENERGY, v21
The clever combatant looks to the effect of combined energy, and does not require too much from individuals.

## Maxim 99 — Chapter V: ENERGY, v22
When he utilises combined energy, his fighting men become as it were like unto rolling logs or stones. For it is the nature of a log or stone to remain motionless on level ground, and to move when on a slope; if four-cornered, to come to a standstill, but if round-shaped, to go rolling down.

## Maxim 100 — Chapter V: ENERGY, v23
Thus the energy developed by good fighting men is as the momentum of a round stone rolled down a mountain thousands of feet in height. So much on the subject of energy.

## Maxim 101 — Chapter VI: WEAK POINTS AND STRONG, v1
Sun Tzŭ said: Whoever is first in the field and awaits the coming of the enemy, will be fresh for the fight; whoever is second in the field and has to hasten to battle, will arrive exhausted.

## Maxim 102 — Chapter VI: WEAK POINTS AND STRONG, v2
Therefore the clever combatant imposes his will on the enemy, but does not allow the enemy’s will to be imposed on him.

## Maxim 103 — Chapter VI: WEAK POINTS AND STRONG, v3
By holding out advantages to him, he can cause the enemy to approach of his own accord; or, by inflicting damage, he can make it impossible for the enemy to draw near.

## Maxim 104 — Chapter VI: WEAK POINTS AND STRONG, v4
If the enemy is taking his ease, he can harass him;

## Maxim 105 — Chapter VI: WEAK POINTS AND STRONG, v5
Appear at points which the enemy must hasten to defend; march swiftly to places where you are not expected.

## Maxim 106 — Chapter VI: WEAK POINTS AND STRONG, v6
An army may march great distances without distress, if it marches through country where the enemy is not.

## Maxim 107 — Chapter VI: WEAK POINTS AND STRONG, v7
You can be sure of succeeding in your attacks if you only attack places which are undefended.

## Maxim 108 — Chapter VI: WEAK POINTS AND STRONG, v8
Hence that general is skilful in attack whose opponent does not know what to defend; and he is skilful in defence whose opponent does not know what to attack.

## Maxim 109 — Chapter VI: WEAK POINTS AND STRONG, v9
O divine art of subtlety and secrecy! Through you we learn to be invisible, through you inaudible;

## Maxim 110 — Chapter VI: WEAK POINTS AND STRONG, v10
You may advance and be absolutely irresistible, if you make for the enemy’s weak points; you may retire and be safe from pursuit if your movements are more rapid than those of the enemy.

## Maxim 111 — Chapter VI: WEAK POINTS AND STRONG, v11
If we wish to fight, the enemy can be forced to an engagement even though he be sheltered behind a high rampart and a deep ditch. All we need do is attack some other place that he will be obliged to relieve.

## Maxim 112 — Chapter VI: WEAK POINTS AND STRONG, v12
If we do not wish to fight, we can prevent the enemy from engaging us even though the lines of our encampment be merely traced out on the ground. All we need do is to throw something odd and unaccountable in his way.

## Maxim 113 — Chapter VI: WEAK POINTS AND STRONG, v13
By discovering the enemy’s dispositions and remaining invisible ourselves, we can keep our forces concentrated, while the enemy’s must be divided.

## Maxim 114 — Chapter VI: WEAK POINTS AND STRONG, v14
We can form a single united body, while the enemy must split up into fractions. Hence there will be a whole pitted against separate parts of a whole, which means that we shall be many to the enemy’s few.

## Maxim 115 — Chapter VI: WEAK POINTS AND STRONG, v15
And if we are able thus to attack an inferior force with a superior one, our opponents will be in dire straits.

## Maxim 116 — Chapter VI: WEAK POINTS AND STRONG, v16
The spot where we intend to fight must not be made known; for then the enemy will have to prepare against a possible attack at several different points;

## Maxim 117 — Chapter VI: WEAK POINTS AND STRONG, v17
For should the enemy strengthen his van, he will weaken his rear; should he strengthen his rear, he will weaken his van; should he strengthen his left, he will weaken his right; should he strengthen his right, he will weaken his left. If he sends reinforcements everywhere, he will everywhere be weak.

## Maxim 118 — Chapter VI: WEAK POINTS AND STRONG, v18
Numerical weakness comes from having to prepare against possible attacks; numerical strength, from compelling our adversary to make these preparations against us.

## Maxim 119 — Chapter VI: WEAK POINTS AND STRONG, v19
Knowing the place and the time of the coming battle, we may concentrate from the greatest distances in order to fight.

## Maxim 120 — Chapter VI: WEAK POINTS AND STRONG, v20
But if neither time nor place be known, then the left wing will be impotent to succour the right, the right equally impotent to succour the left, the van unable to relieve the rear, or the rear to support the van. How much more so if the furthest portions of the army are anything under a hundred li apart, and even the nearest are separated by several li!

## Maxim 121 — Chapter VI: WEAK POINTS AND STRONG, v21
Though according to my estimate the soldiers of Yüeh exceed our own in number, that shall advantage them nothing in the matter of victory. I say then that victory can be achieved.

## Maxim 122 — Chapter VI: WEAK POINTS AND STRONG, v22
Though the enemy be stronger in numbers, we may prevent him from fighting. Scheme so as to discover his plans and the likelihood of their success.

## Maxim 123 — Chapter VI: WEAK POINTS AND STRONG, v23
Rouse him, and learn the principle of his activity or inactivity.

## Maxim 124 — Chapter VI: WEAK POINTS AND STRONG, v24
Carefully compare the opposing army with your own, so that you may know where strength is superabundant and where it is deficient.

## Maxim 125 — Chapter VI: WEAK POINTS AND STRONG, v25
In making tactical dispositions, the highest pitch you can attain is to conceal them;

## Maxim 126 — Chapter VI: WEAK POINTS AND STRONG, v26
How victory may be produced for them out of the enemy’s own tactics—that is what the multitude cannot comprehend.

## Maxim 127 — Chapter VI: WEAK POINTS AND STRONG, v27
All men can see the tactics whereby I conquer, but what none can see is the strategy out of which victory is evolved.

## Maxim 128 — Chapter VI: WEAK POINTS AND STRONG, v28
Do not repeat the tactics which have gained you one victory, but let your methods be regulated by the infinite variety of circumstances.

## Maxim 129 — Chapter VI: WEAK POINTS AND STRONG, v29
Military tactics are like unto water; for water in its natural course runs away from high places and hastens downwards.

## Maxim 130 — Chapter VI: WEAK POINTS AND STRONG, v30
So in war, the way is to avoid what is strong and to strike at what is weak.

## Maxim 131 — Chapter VI: WEAK POINTS AND STRONG, v31
Water shapes its course according to the nature of the ground over which it flows; the soldier works out his victory in relation to the foe whom he is facing.

## Maxim 132 — Chapter VI: WEAK POINTS AND STRONG, v32
Therefore, just as water retains no constant shape, so in warfare there are no constant conditions.

## Maxim 133 — Chapter VI: WEAK POINTS AND STRONG, v33
He who can modify his tactics in relation to his opponent and thereby succeed in winning, may be called a heaven-born captain.

## Maxim 135 — Chapter VII: MANŒUVERING, v1
Sun Tzŭ said: In war, the general receives his commands from the sovereign.

## Maxim 136 — Chapter VII: MANŒUVERING, v2
Having collected an army and concentrated his forces, he must blend and harmonise the different elements thereof before pitching his camp.

## Maxim 137 — Chapter VII: MANŒUVERING, v3
After that, comes tactical manœuvering, than which there is nothing more difficult.

## Maxim 138 — Chapter VII: MANŒUVERING, v4
Thus, to take a long and circuitous route, after enticing the enemy out of the way, and though starting after him, to contrive to reach the goal before him, shows knowledge of the artifice of deviation.

## Maxim 139 — Chapter VII: MANŒUVERING, v5
Manœuvering with an army is advantageous; with an undisciplined multitude, most dangerous.

## Maxim 140 — Chapter VII: MANŒUVERING, v6
If you set a fully equipped army in march in order to snatch an advantage, the chances are that you will be too late. On the other hand, to detach a flying column for the purpose involves the sacrifice of its baggage and stores.

Thus, if you order your men to roll up their buff-coats, and make forced marches without halting day or night, covering double the usual distance at a stretch,

The stronger men will be in front, the jaded ones will fall behind, and on this plan only one-tenth of your army will reach its destination.

## Maxim 143 — Chapter VII: MANŒUVERING, v9
If you march fifty li in order to outmanœuver the enemy, you will lose the leader of your first division, and only half your force will reach the goal.

## Maxim 144 — Chapter VII: MANŒUVERING, v10
If you march thirty li with the same object, two-thirds of your army will arrive.

## Maxim 145 — Chapter VII: MANŒUVERING, v11
We may take it then that an army without its baggage-train is lost; without provisions it is lost; without bases of supply it is lost.

## Maxim 146 — Chapter VII: MANŒUVERING, v12
We cannot enter into alliances until we are acquainted with the designs of our neighbours.

## Maxim 147 — Chapter VII: MANŒUVERING, v13
We are not fit to lead an army on the march unless we are familiar with the face of the country—its mountains and forests, its pitfalls and precipices, its marshes and swamps.

## Maxim 148 — Chapter VII: MANŒUVERING, v14
We shall be unable to turn natural advantages to account unless we make use of local guides.

## Maxim 149 — Chapter VII: MANŒUVERING, v15
In war, practise dissimulation, and you will succeed.

## Maxim 150 — Chapter VII: MANŒUVERING, v16
Whether to concentrate or to divide your troops, must be decided by circumstances.

## Maxim 151 — Chapter VII: MANŒUVERING, v17
Let your rapidity be that of the wind,

## Maxim 152 — Chapter VII: MANŒUVERING, v18
In raiding and plundering be like fire,

## Maxim 153 — Chapter VII: MANŒUVERING, v19
Let your plans be dark and impenetrable as night, and when you move, fall like a thunderbolt.

## Maxim 154 — Chapter VII: MANŒUVERING, v20
When you plunder a countryside, let the spoil be divided amongst your men;

## Maxim 155 — Chapter VII: MANŒUVERING, v21
Ponder and deliberate before you make a move.

## Maxim 156 — Chapter VII: MANŒUVERING, v22
He will conquer who has learnt the artifice of deviation.

Gongs and drums, banners and flags, are means whereby the ears and eyes of the host may be focussed on one particular point.

## Maxim 159 — Chapter VII: MANŒUVERING, v25
The host thus forming a single united body, is it impossible either for the brave to advance alone, or for the cowardly to retreat alone.

## Maxim 160 — Chapter VII: MANŒUVERING, v26
In night-fighting, then, make much use of signal-fires and drums, and in fighting by day, of flags and banners, as a means of influencing the ears and eyes of your army.

A whole army may be robbed of its spirit;

## Maxim 162 — Chapter VII: MANŒUVERING, v28
Now a soldier’s spirit is keenest in the morning;

## Maxim 163 — Chapter VII: MANŒUVERING, v29
A clever general, therefore, avoids an army when its spirit is keen, but attacks it when it is sluggish and inclined to return. This is the art of studying moods.

## Maxim 164 — Chapter VII: MANŒUVERING, v30
Disciplined and calm, to await the appearance of disorder and hubbub amongst the enemy:—this is the art of retaining self-possession.

## Maxim 165 — Chapter VII: MANŒUVERING, v31
To be near the goal while the enemy is still far from it, to wait at ease while the enemy is toiling and struggling, to be well-fed while the enemy is famished:—this is the art of husbanding one’s strength.

## Maxim 166 — Chapter VII: MANŒUVERING, v32

## Maxim 342 — Chapter XII: THE ATTACK BY FIRE, v21

## Maxim 343 — Chapter XII: THE ATTACK BY FIRE, v22

## Maxim 344 — Chapter XIII: THE USE OF SPIES, v1
Sun Tzŭ said: Raising a host of a hundred thousand men and marching them great distances entails heavy loss on the people and a drain on the resources of the State. The daily expenditure will amount to a thousand ounces of silver.

## Maxim 345 — Chapter XIII: THE USE OF SPIES, v2
Hostile armies may face each other for years, striving for the victory which is decided in a single day. This being so, to remain in ignorance of the enemy’s condition simply because one grudges the outlay of a hundred ounces of silver in honours and emoluments,

## Maxim 346 — Chapter XIII: THE USE OF SPIES, v3
One who acts thus is no leader of men, no present help to his sovereign, no master of victory.

## Maxim 347 — Chapter XIII: THE USE OF SPIES, v4
Thus, what enables the wise sovereign and the good general to strike and conquer, and achieve things beyond the reach of ordinary men, is foreknowledge.

## Maxim 348 — Chapter XIII: THE USE OF SPIES, v5
Now this foreknowledge cannot be elicited from spirits; it cannot be obtained inductively from experience,

## Maxim 349 — Chapter XIII: THE USE OF SPIES, v6
Knowledge of the enemy’s dispositions can only be obtained from other men.

## Maxim 350 — Chapter XIII: THE USE OF SPIES, v7
Hence the use of spies, of whom there are five classes: (1) Local spies; (2) inward spies; (3) converted spies; (4) doomed spies; (5) surviving spies.

## Maxim 351 — Chapter XIII: THE USE OF SPIES, v8
When these five kinds of spy are all at work, none can discover the secret system. This is called "divine manipulation of the threads." It is the sovereign’s most precious faculty.

## Maxim 352 — Chapter XIII: THE USE OF SPIES, v9
Having local spies means employing the services of the inhabitants of a district.

## Maxim 353 — Chapter XIII: THE USE OF SPIES, v10
Having inward spies, making use of officials of the enemy.

## Maxim 354 — Chapter XIII: THE USE OF SPIES, v11
Having converted spies, getting hold of the enemy’s spies and using them for our own purposes.

## Maxim 355 — Chapter XIII: THE USE OF SPIES, v12
Having doomed spies, doing certain things openly for purposes of deception, and allowing our own spies to know of them and report them to the enemy.

## Maxim 356 — Chapter XIII: THE USE OF SPIES, v13
Surviving spies, finally, are those who bring back news from the enemy’s camp.

## Maxim 357 — Chapter XIII: THE USE OF SPIES, v14
Hence it is that with none in the whole army are more intimate relations to be maintained than with spies.

## Maxim 358 — Chapter XIII: THE USE OF SPIES, v15
Spies cannot be usefully employed without a certain intuitive sagacity.

## Maxim 359 — Chapter XIII: THE USE OF SPIES, v16
They cannot be properly managed without benevolence and straightforwardness.

## Maxim 360 — Chapter XIII: THE USE OF SPIES, v17
Without subtle ingenuity of mind, one cannot make certain of the truth of their reports.

## Maxim 361 — Chapter XIII: THE USE OF SPIES, v18
Be subtle! be subtle! and use your spies for every kind of business.

## Maxim 362 — Chapter XIII: THE USE OF SPIES, v19
If a secret piece of news is divulged by a spy before the time is ripe, he must be put to death together with the man to whom the secret was told.

## Maxim 363 — Chapter XIII: THE USE OF SPIES, v20
Whether the object be to crush an army, to storm a city, or to assassinate an individual, it is always necessary to begin by finding out the names of the attendants, the aides-de- camp,

## Maxim 364 — Chapter XIII: THE USE OF SPIES, v21
The enemy’s spies who have come to spy on us must be sought out, tempted with bribes, led away and comfortably housed. Thus they will become converted spies and available for our service.

## Maxim 365 — Chapter XIII: THE USE OF SPIES, v22
It is through the information brought by the converted spy that we are able to acquire and employ local and inward spies.

## Maxim 366 — Chapter XIII: THE USE OF SPIES, v23
It is owing to his information, again, that we can cause the doomed spy to carry false tidings to the enemy.

## Maxim 367 — Chapter XIII: THE USE OF SPIES, v24
Lastly, it is by his information that the surviving spy can be used on appointed occasions.

## Maxim 368 — Chapter XIII: THE USE OF SPIES, v25
The end and aim of spying in all its five varieties is knowledge of the enemy; and this knowledge can only be derived, in the first instance, from the converted spy.
