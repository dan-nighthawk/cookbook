## Scene 2 — The Guard Looks Away
**Leading character:** Nymphidius Sabinus
**Dialog:** "Galba is emperor now — the Guard stands with him"

The Castra Praetoria, the Praetorian barracks northeast of Rome — night. The image is sepia: ranked soldiers in firelit half-darkness, lorica segmentata catching the lamplight in pale bands. Color enters first in the faces. The sharp, weathered expression of Nymphidius Sabinus, joint Praetorian Prefect, takes on warmth and resolution. This is a man who has performed his calculation and arrived at his answer.

Word has come from the provinces. Galba, governor of Hispania Tarraconensis, has marched on Rome and declared himself emperor. Nymphidius has already sent his message of allegiance — carrying the Guard's loyalty as a gift to the advancing general, in exchange for promises of reward. All that remains is the announcement.

He mounts a low stone platform before his assembled cohort. The men are the finest soldiers in the empire, sworn protectors of the throne. Thrones, Nymphidius knows, can change masters. He speaks and watches the ripple move through the ranks: uncertainty, exchange of glances, and then — most men — a slow nod. They follow power. Power has shifted. A low sound, almost like relief, moves through the barracks. Nero, alone in his palace on the Palatine, hears nothing. No one has thought to tell him yet.

## Scene 4 — What an Artist
**Leading character:** Nero
**Dialog:** "What an artist the world is losing in me!"

The small interior of Phaon's villa — earthen floor, rough stone walls, a single oil lamp guttering in a draft. The image is in full, saturated color here: the warm amber of lamplight on pale stone, the glint of a blade. Nero crouches near the far wall. Around him, three or four freedmen stand helpless. From beyond the villa's walls comes the distant sound of hoofbeats — riders sent by the Senate to take him alive.

Hold on his face. This is not wholly the figure of senatorial pamphlets. He is a man who wept before signing death warrants and signed them anyway; who performed on stage before thousands because he genuinely believed in his own art; who loved Greece and left Rome once to be applauded in its theaters. He is also the man who murdered his mother Agrippina and his wife Octavia. He is, entirely, what the Julio-Claudian dynasty became.

The blade trembles in his grip. He raises it to his throat — and cannot complete the act alone. A freedman, Epaphroditus, moves quietly to his side and places a steadying hand over his. The lamp shudders. Outside, the hoofbeats grow louder. Nero draws one long, ragged breath and speaks — his voice thin, his conviction, for this one last moment, absolute.

## Scene 6 — The Year Cracks Open
**Leading character:** Galba
**Dialog:** "Rome has endured the tyrant long enough — now she breathes"

A road into Rome, weeks later — but the image intercuts: sepia photographs of Galba's iron-hard, seventy-year-old face; a color panorama of the city from the Palatine Hill, smoke still threading up from quarters unsettled by Nero's final days. Galba rides at the head of his column. He is the first of what will become four emperors in a single year — a fact no one in this scene yet knows.

Around him Rome is already fracturing. In the Rhine camps, Vitellius weighs his chances. Otho, who backed Galba's rise, grows quietly impatient. The Senate that voted Nero a public enemy now swears loyalty to the new man with the same mechanical unanimity it has extended to every emperor before him. The Praetorian Guard that abandoned one emperor for a reward awaits the delivery of that reward. These are not foundations; they are fault lines.

Galba does not look triumphant. He looks tired — the tiredness of an old soldier who has crossed a threshold he cannot uncross. The camera pulls high and wide as he enters the city gates: Rome immense below, marble and tenement and temple and squalor, a million people going about an uncertain day. The dynasty of Augustus is gone. Power, it turns out, belongs to whoever the legions say it belongs to. The next man who proves that lesson will not be Galba.

Behind him, Rome catches the early sun. From here it could be any century. The Chronicler's expression carries the weight it always carries at this moment: not satisfaction, not mourning exactly, but the gravity of a day whose consequences are still being counted.

What Nero's death ended was not merely a man — however complicated, however destructive, however gifted in his own estimation. What ended was the idea that an emperor's legitimacy derived from blood, from the chain back to Augustus, from inheritance as a governing principle. The Julio-Claudians had held that idea together for a century, through genius and madness and murder. On a dirt floor outside Rome, on this morning, it came apart.

What replaced it was simpler and more brutal: the emperor is whoever the armies choose to make emperor. Rome would spend the next year learning exactly what that meant — and the centuries after that trying to forget.
