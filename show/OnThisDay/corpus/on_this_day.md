# On This Day — Corpus (Historical Anniversaries)

Source: well-established, public-domain historical record. Each entry states a
single notable event tied to a calendar date (`MM-DD`), written from the factual
account in original prose (historical *facts* are not copyrightable). One
anniversary per episode; `compute.js` keys off today's `MM-DD`, then asks the
model to stage that event as a dramatized reenactment framed by The Chronicler.

Entry format (parsed by `compute.js`):

    ## MM-DD — YEAR — <short title>
    <one factual paragraph: who, what, where, why it mattered>

Dates may carry more than one entry; `compute.js` rotates among them by episode
count so a date used in successive years need not repeat. If today's date has no
entry yet, `compute.js` walks forward to the next date that does — so the corpus
can be filled in over time without ever breaking a daily render. Keep every added
account to genuinely public-domain history (pre-1929 events are safest; modern
events are fine when stated as plain fact).

---

## 01-15 — 1559 — Elizabeth I is crowned Queen of England
On 15 January 1559 Elizabeth Tudor was crowned in Westminster Abbey, beginning a
forty-four-year reign that would be remembered as a golden age of English drama,
exploration and naval power. The daughter of Henry VIII and Anne Boleyn had spent
years in danger and disgrace; her coronation opened the Elizabethan era.

## 01-27 — 1756 — Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart is born in Salzburg
On 27 January 1756 a son was born to the court musician Leopold Mozart in
Salzburg. A prodigy who composed before he could properly write, Wolfgang Amadeus
Mozart would reshape the symphony, the concerto and the opera within a short life
of thirty-five years, leaving more than six hundred works.

## 02-12 — 1809 — Abraham Lincoln and Charles Darwin are both born
By coincidence of the calendar, two men who would each transform their world were
born on the same day, 12 February 1809: Abraham Lincoln in a Kentucky log cabin,
and Charles Darwin into an English physician's family. One would preserve a union
and end its slavery; the other would change how humanity understood its own origin.

## 02-15 — 1564 — Galileo Galilei is born in Pisa
On 15 February 1564 Galileo Galilei was born in Pisa. He would turn a newly
improved telescope toward the night sky, discover the moons of Jupiter and the
phases of Venus, and insist that the Earth moved around the Sun — a claim that
brought him before the Inquisition but laid a cornerstone of modern science.

## 03-15 — 0044 — The assassination of Julius Caesar
On the Ides of March, 15 March 44 BC, Gaius Julius Caesar was struck down by a
conspiracy of Roman senators at the foot of Pompey's statue. Fearing he meant to
make himself king, men he had pardoned and promoted closed around him with daggers.
His death did not restore the Republic; it ushered in the age of emperors.

## 04-14 — 1865 — Abraham Lincoln is shot at Ford's Theatre
On the night of 14 April 1865, only days after the surrender that ended the
American Civil War, President Abraham Lincoln was shot while watching a play at
Ford's Theatre in Washington. He died the next morning. The man who had held the
Union together fell at the very hour of its victory.

## 04-18 — 1906 — The Great San Francisco Earthquake
Before dawn on 18 April 1906 a violent earthquake struck San Francisco, and the
fires that followed burned for days. Much of the city was destroyed and hundreds
of thousands were left homeless. The disaster reshaped how engineers and cities
thought about building to survive the shaking of the earth.

## 04-26 — 1564 — William Shakespeare is baptized at Stratford
On 26 April 1564 a child named William was baptized at Holy Trinity Church in
Stratford-upon-Avon. The glover's son would become the most performed playwright
in any language, giving the stage Hamlet, Lear and a thousand phrases that English
speakers still use without knowing their author.

## 05-06 — 1840 — The Penny Black, the first postage stamp, goes into use
On 6 May 1840 the world's first adhesive postage stamp, the Penny Black bearing
the profile of Queen Victoria, became valid for use in Britain. A single penny now
carried a letter anywhere in the kingdom, and the simple idea of prepaid, uniform
postage soon circled the globe.

## 05-20 — 1506 — Christopher Columbus dies at Valladolid
On 20 May 1506 Christopher Columbus died in Valladolid, Spain, still insisting he
had reached the edge of Asia. His four voyages across the Atlantic had instead
opened sustained contact between Europe and the Americas — a collision of worlds
whose consequences, for good and ill, would remake both hemispheres.

## 05-29 — 1453 — The fall of Constantinople
On 29 May 1453, after a siege of fifty-three days, the army of the Ottoman sultan
Mehmed II breached the great walls of Constantinople. The last emperor of the
Romans fell with his city, ending an empire that had endured for a thousand years
and sending scholars and manuscripts westward into a quickening Renaissance.

## 06-05 — 1783 — The Montgolfier brothers fly the first hot-air balloon
On 5 June 1783, in the French town of Annonay, Joseph and Étienne Montgolfier sent
an unmanned linen-and-paper balloon high into the air over an astonished crowd.
Heated air alone had lifted a craft from the ground. Within months human beings
would ride such balloons aloft, and the dream of flight had its first proof.

## 06-08 — 0793 — The Viking raid on Lindisfarne
On 8 June 793 Norse raiders fell upon the island monastery of Lindisfarne off the
coast of Northumbria, plundering its treasures and slaughtering monks. Chroniclers
recorded it with horror as an omen; historians mark it as the opening of the Viking
Age, three centuries in which Norse seafarers reshaped the map of Europe.

## 06-09 — 0068 — The Emperor Nero dies, ending the Julio-Claudian line
On 9 June AD 68 Nero, the last emperor of the dynasty founded by Augustus, took his
own life on the outskirts of Rome. Abandoned by the Senate, the Praetorian Guard and
his own court, and declared a public enemy, he died with the reported lament that the
world was losing a great artist. His death plunged Rome into a year of four emperors.

## 06-09 — 1934 — Donald Duck makes his screen debut
On 9 June 1934 a short-tempered duck in a sailor suit waddled onto cinema screens
in the animated film "The Wise Little Hen." Donald Duck, with his explosive temper
and barely intelligible quack, quickly rivaled Mickey Mouse himself and became one
of the most enduring characters in animation.

## 06-15 — 1215 — King John seals Magna Carta at Runnymede
On 15 June 1215, in the meadow of Runnymede beside the Thames, the rebellious barons
of England forced King John to set his seal to Magna Carta. The charter bound even
the king beneath the law and promised that no free man should be imprisoned save by
lawful judgment of his peers — a seed of liberties that would outgrow its own century.

## 06-18 — 1815 — The Battle of Waterloo
On 18 June 1815, on muddy fields south of Brussels, the armies of Britain and Prussia
under Wellington and Blücher broke the forces of Napoleon Bonaparte. The defeat ended
the emperor's astonishing hundred-day return from exile and closed more than two decades
of war that had convulsed the whole of Europe.

## 06-28 — 1914 — The assassination at Sarajevo
On 28 June 1914 a young Bosnian Serb named Gavrilo Princip shot the Archduke Franz
Ferdinand, heir to the Austro-Hungarian throne, and his wife Sophie in the streets of
Sarajevo. Within six weeks the tangled alliances of Europe had turned two pistol shots
into the First World War.

## 07-04 — 1776 — The American Declaration of Independence is adopted
On 4 July 1776 the Continental Congress in Philadelphia adopted the Declaration of
Independence, proclaiming that the thirteen colonies were free of British rule and that
all men are created equal. The words, largely drafted by Thomas Jefferson, would echo
far beyond the war that secured them.

## 07-14 — 1789 — The storming of the Bastille
On 14 July 1789 the people of Paris stormed the Bastille, a royal fortress and prison
that had come to symbolize the arbitrary power of the French crown. The fall of the old
stronghold became the rallying image of the French Revolution and is still marked each
year as the birthday of the Republic.

## 07-20 — 1969 — Apollo 11 lands the first humans on the Moon
On 20 July 1969 the lunar module Eagle settled onto the Sea of Tranquility and Neil
Armstrong stepped from its ladder onto the surface of the Moon, calling it one small
step for a man and one giant leap for mankind. For the first time, human beings stood
on another world.

## 08-06 — 1945 — The atomic bombing of Hiroshima
On the morning of 6 August 1945 a single American aircraft dropped an atomic bomb on the
Japanese city of Hiroshima, destroying it in a flash and killing tens of thousands at
once. The dawn of nuclear weapons hastened the end of the Second World War and cast a
long shadow over every decade that followed.

## 08-24 — 0079 — Vesuvius buries Pompeii and Herculaneum
In the late summer of AD 79 the volcano Vesuvius erupted, burying the Roman towns of
Pompeii and Herculaneum under ash and rock. Whole streets, houses and people were sealed
where they stood. Centuries later the buried cities would be unearthed, frozen portraits
of a single Roman day.

## 09-02 — 1666 — The Great Fire of London begins
On 2 September 1666 a fire that started in a baker's shop on Pudding Lane spread through
the timber houses of London, burning for four days until much of the medieval city lay in
ashes. From the ruin rose a new London of brick and stone, and the dome of a rebuilt
St Paul's.

## 10-12 — 1492 — Columbus's expedition sights land in the Americas
In the small hours of 12 October 1492 a lookout aboard one of Columbus's three ships
cried out at the sight of land — an island in the Bahamas. The crossing opened a
permanent, world-altering contact between Europe and the Americas, for the peoples of
both, the beginning of an utterly changed age.

## 10-31 — 1517 — Martin Luther posts the Ninety-Five Theses
On 31 October 1517, by tradition, the monk Martin Luther nailed his Ninety-Five Theses
to the door of the castle church in Wittenberg, attacking the sale of indulgences. The
challenge spread on the new printing presses and split Western Christianity, igniting the
Protestant Reformation.

## 11-09 — 1989 — The fall of the Berlin Wall
On the night of 9 November 1989 a confused announcement sent crowds of East Berliners to
the checkpoints of the wall that had divided their city for twenty-eight years. The guards,
overwhelmed, let them through, and Berliners climbed the concrete to embrace across it. The
Cold War's starkest symbol fell in a single night.

## 11-19 — 1863 — Lincoln delivers the Gettysburg Address
On 19 November 1863, at the dedication of a cemetery on the battlefield of Gettysburg,
President Abraham Lincoln spoke for barely two minutes. In a few hundred words he recast the
Civil War as a test of whether a nation conceived in liberty could endure, and resolved that
government of, by and for the people should not perish from the earth.

## 12-17 — 1903 — The Wright brothers achieve powered flight
On 17 December 1903, on the windswept dunes of Kitty Hawk, North Carolina, Orville and
Wilbur Wright flew a powered, heavier-than-air machine for twelve seconds. It was the first
controlled flight of its kind, and within a single lifetime it would carry humanity from a
sand dune to the edge of space.

## 12-25 — 0800 — Charlemagne is crowned Emperor in Rome
On Christmas Day in the year 800, in St Peter's Basilica in Rome, Pope Leo III placed a crown
on the head of the Frankish king Charles, hailing him as Emperor of the Romans. The coronation
of Charlemagne revived the imperial title in the West and bound together church and crown for
centuries to come.
